Course Overview

This blended learning module introduces GS1 standards and explores the critical aspects of data interoperability in modern business systems. Designed for business professionals, commercial organisations and students, the course combines lectures, hands‑on labs and case studies over 8–10 weeks (≈40–50 hours). Participants should have a basic understanding of business processes and information systems.

Module 1: Introduction to GS1 Standards

GS1 is a global organisation that maintains identification standards used across supply chains. Its standards enable businesses to uniquely identify products, locations and assets, capture that information in barcodes or RFID tags and share it with trading partners[2]. The primary identification keys include the Global Trade Item Number (GTIN), Serial Shipping Container Code (SSCC), Global Location Number (GLN) and Global Individual Asset Identifier (GIAI). The GTIN uniquely identifies trade items such as products or services that are priced, ordered or invoiced at any point in the supply chain[1].

Universal standards reduce duplication, support automation and improve traceability across global markets. For example, Walmart requires suppliers to obtain a GS1 Company Prefix so that each item can be uniquely identified in their systems[3]. Organisations adopting GS1 standards report improved supply‑chain efficiency, inventory management and regulatory compliance[2].

Module 2: EPCIS – Electronic Product Code Information Services

Electronic Product Code Information Services (EPCIS) is an open standard for capturing and sharing supply‑chain event data. It defines a data model and standardised capture and query interfaces so that disparate applications can create and share visibility event data across enterprises[4]. EPCIS 2.0 introduces support for GS1 Digital Link and adds an AssociationEvent, improving value‑chain visibility, traceability and efficiency while reducing compliance costs[5].

EPCIS events follow the “What, Where, When, Why” framework: they record what objects were involved, where the event took place, when it happened and why (e.g., which business process or transaction)[8]. The main event types include:

  • ObjectEvent – Observations about individual items or batches.
  • AggregationEvent – Relationships between items and logistic units (e.g., cases or pallets).
  • TransactionEvent – Associations between items and business transactions such as purchase orders or invoices.
  • TransformationEvent – Records changes in product identity, for example during manufacturing.
  • AssociationEvent – New in EPCIS 2.0; links objects to non‑EPCIS entities, such as digital link resources[6].

Because EPCIS provides a consistent data model and standard APIs, it reduces integration costs and fosters interoperability among trading partners[7].

Module 3: Data‑Link Technologies

GS1 Digital Link bridges the physical and digital worlds by turning barcodes and other data carriers into web links. A digital link URI embeds a product’s identifier (such as a GTIN) into a web address, allowing anyone to scan a 2D barcode and retrieve rich, real‑time information about the product[9]. Digital Link URIs follow a precise syntax beginning with the domain, followed by the identifier type and optional qualifiers[10]. Resolvers map these URIs to resources, enabling omnichannel experiences, supply‑chain visibility and regulatory compliance[9].

Digital Link is driving the transition from traditional 1D barcodes to high‑capacity 2D codes. By 2027 (“Sunrise 2027”), industries aim to adopt 2D barcodes universally so that a single symbol can carry a product’s identity and a link to dynamic data. Proof‑of‑concept projects by brands and retailers show how digital link can provide allergen information, recall status and marketing content on demand[12]. The technology supports brand loyalty programmes, supply‑chain traceability and consumer engagement[11].

Module 4: Foundations of Interoperability

Interoperability refers to the ability of systems and organisations to work together seamlessly. An effective interoperability model considers multiple layers: legal (laws, contracts and privacy), organisational (governance and workflows), semantic (shared meaning) and technical (protocols and data formats)[13]. Syntactic interoperability allows systems to exchange data via common formats (e.g., XML or JSON), whereas semantic interoperability ensures that exchanged data can be understood meaningfully using shared vocabularies and ontologies[14].

The Information Systems Interoperability Maturity Model (ISIMM) helps organisations benchmark their interoperability maturity across technical, organisational and semantic dimensions and develop strategies for improvement[15]. Without interoperability, redundancies and inefficiencies proliferate; in healthcare alone, a lack of interoperability is estimated to cost more than US$30 billion per year[16].

Interoperability also intersects with legal and regulatory frameworks. Policy recommendations for cross‑border data flows emphasise agreeing on common data privacy principles, developing common data classifications, using privacy‑enhancing technologies and maintaining up‑to‑date repositories of regulations[17]. Organisations operating under the EU’s General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) must ensure that cross‑border data transfers rely on adequacy decisions, standard contractual clauses or binding corporate rules, and should implement best practices such as mapping data flows, conducting data‑protection impact assessments, strengthening data governance and training staff[18].

Standards development involves intellectual‑property considerations. Standards (e.g., Bluetooth, USB) are often composed of patents held by multiple entities. Holders of standard‑essential patents agree to licence them on fair, reasonable and non‑discriminatory (FRAND) terms, but interpretations of FRAND are contested and require governance mechanisms[19].

Module 5: Non‑Technical Aspects of Interoperability

Achieving interoperability is not solely a technical challenge. Organisational factors include cultural resistance to change, misaligned processes and the need for skills and training. Legal and regulatory requirements (e.g., privacy laws, data localisation rules and intellectual property) must be respected[17][18]. Economic considerations require evaluating costs and benefits: while implementing interoperable systems may require upfront investment, failing to interoperate can result in significant inefficiencies and costs (as demonstrated in healthcare)[16]. Successful projects engage stakeholders early, build consensus and establish clear governance structures.

Module 6: Semantics and Interoperability

Semantic interoperability ensures that exchanged information carries the same meaning across systems. It builds upon syntactic interoperability by using shared vocabularies, taxonomies, thesauri, ontologies and knowledge graphs[14]. A vocabulary service standard seeks to unify how controlled vocabularies, taxonomies and ontologies are shared via APIs, emphasising governance, provenance and federation to support semantic interoperability across distributed systems[21].

Organisations often manage numerous ontologies and taxonomies. A case study in the pharmaceutical sector showed that adopting an ontology management system and pushing standardised vocabularies via APIs created a single source of truth and improved research efficiency and FAIR data management[20]. Semantic mapping techniques (schema matching, data transformations and ontology alignment) help translate between different data models, enabling cross‑industry data exchange and AI‑driven analytics.

Module 7: Standards & Interoperability

The standards landscape includes international bodies such as ISO, W3C and IEEE, as well as industry‑specific organisations. Choosing the right standards requires aligning business requirements, evaluating compatibility and considering future‑proofing. Technical standards cover data formats (XML, JSON, CSV), communication protocols (REST, SOAP, GraphQL) and security frameworks (OAuth, SAML).

Understanding communication protocols is critical. SOAP is a protocol with strict rules, robust security and built‑in error handling; it exclusively uses XML and suits enterprise systems requiring reliability and formal contracts. REST is a lightweight architectural style that leverages HTTP methods and supports multiple data formats (JSON, XML) and scales easily for microservices and web APIs[22]. Developers should choose SOAP for complex tasks needing strict security and REST for flexible, lightweight applications.

Security frameworks like OAuth allow users to grant applications access to resources without sharing credentials. OAuth 2.1 emphasises improved security practices (e.g., PKCE, removal of implicit flows) and defines roles such as resource owner, client, resource server and authorisation server[23].

Standards selection should also account for intellectual‑property considerations: standards often incorporate patented technologies, and fair, reasonable and non‑discriminatory (FRAND) licensing terms are essential to ensure broad adoption while respecting innovators’ rights[19].

Module 8: Reference Model Agro (rmAgro)

In the agrifood sector, innovation is frequently hindered by fragmented data and poor interoperability. The reference model Agro (rmAgro) provides standardised data models, architectures and services to optimise data flows along the food value chain. Wageningen Research uses rmAgro to align with industry standards, test against use cases and support AI‑driven solutions for traceability, food safety and sustainability[28].

Implementing rmAgro involves configuring core components, deploying reference schemas and integrating with existing systems. Use cases include crop management, livestock tracking, food safety compliance and sustainability reporting.

Module 9: Semantic Modelling in Composer

TopBraid Composer is a visual modelling environment for creating and managing domain models and ontologies in RDF, RDFS and OWL. It offers drag‑and‑drop editing, import of legacy models (UML, XML schemas), reasoning engines (OWL and SPARQL) and integration with the Eclipse/Jena platform[25]. Composer supports controlled vocabularies, version control, collaboration and export to various formats.

Module 10: Semantic Modelling in Protégé

Protégé is a free, open‑source platform for constructing domain models and knowledge‑based applications with ontologies. WebProtégé offers collaborative editing with revision history, a customisable interface and support for OWL 2, enabling teams to work together online. Protégé Desktop provides a feature‑rich editor with reasoning capabilities and integration with reasoners such as HermiT and Pellet, supporting multiple serialisation formats and cross‑compatibility with WebProtégé[24].

Module 11: The World of Linked Data & Ontologies

Linked Data applies web principles to data. Tim Berners‑Lee’s design principles advocate: (1) using URIs as names for things, (2) using HTTP URIs so people can look up those names, (3) providing useful information when URIs are dereferenced using standards like RDF and SPARQL, and (4) linking to other URIs so that more things can be discovered[27]. These principles underpin knowledge graphs and the semantic web.

Numerous ontologies exist, including general‑purpose models (Schema.org, FOAF, Dublin Core) and industry‑specific ones. Building and reusing ontologies enables cross‑domain data integration and lays the foundation for AI and machine‑learning applications. Developing ontologies involves identifying key concepts, classes and relationships, reusing existing vocabularies when possible, and iteratively refining the model; well‑designed ontologies make implicit assumptions explicit and support interoperability[26].

Final Capstone Project

The capstone project allows you to apply the skills acquired in this course. Choose one of the following options:

  1. Supply‑Chain Visibility System: Design and prototype a complete GS1‑based tracking system using EPCIS and Digital Link.
  2. Semantic Integration Platform: Build an interoperability solution using ontologies and semantic mapping.
  3. Industry‑Specific Solution: Develop a comprehensive interoperability strategy for a real organisation.

Deliverables include a technical design document (15–20 pages), proof‑of‑concept implementation, presentation and peer review. Projects are evaluated on technical accuracy, practicality, innovation, documentation quality and presentation effectiveness.
